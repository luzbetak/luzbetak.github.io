California Interview Recording Law

Two-Party (All-Party) Consent Requirement

Under California law, recording a conversation without proper consent is illegal in most circumstances. California Penal Code § 632 makes it a crime to intentionally record or eavesdrop on a confidential communication without the consent of all parties involved.

Key Points

  • California is a two-party (all-party) consent state.
  • Every participant in a private conversation must agree to the recording.
  • This applies to phone calls, video calls, Zoom, Microsoft Teams, Google Meet interviews, and any electronic communication where privacy is reasonably expected.
  • Consent must be explicit or clearly implied; silence or mere participation does not constitute consent.
  • Recording without consent is illegal even if one party is aware of the recording.

What Qualifies as a “Confidential Communication”

  • Any conversation where participants reasonably expect privacy
  • Job interviews
  • Technical screenings
  • Internal corporate calls
  • Remote video interviews

Penalties

  • Criminal penalties (misdemeanor or felony)
  • Fines and/or jail time
  • Civil liability, including the right of the recorded party to sue for damages

Important Clarifications

  • Informing someone after recording has started does not cure the violation.
  • Company policy does not override California law.
  • Being an employer or recruiter does not create an exception.

Relevant Statutes

  • California Penal Code § 632
  • Related sections: § 632.5 and § 637.2 (civil remedies)
